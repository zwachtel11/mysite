Zachary Wachtel

Full Stack Developer. Software Engineer Intern @Qualcomm!

June, 2017 - 5 min read

“UNIX is basically a simple operating system, but you have to be a genius to understand the simplicity.” - Ken Thompson

Zachary Adam Wachtel is a Software Engineer expecting to gain full employment in the field post-graduation. His interests range from the fascinating growing industry of connected devices and the Internet of Things to cloud computing and the infrastructure of modern web applications (cloud databases, APIs, frontend integration, etc.).

Zachary formerly worked at Keen Home, a company whose product, the Smart Vent, allows for customers to regulate the airflow of their homes from their smartphones. At Keen Home, he played a key role in the testing and verification of the Smart Vent and Smart Bridge. Also, Zach developed and deployed an important set of tools which allowed for the company to automate most of the customer support techniques. Currently, he is spending his summer with Qualcomm, as a Software Engineering intern. In his spare time, Zach often loves to tinker with new technologies and spends his time creating side projects which you can check out on his github.

Zach is a diligent engineer with an intensive passion for software. He loves problem-solving oriented work and has a hunger for knowledge, especially in the software field.

He is currently pursuing a double major in Computer Engineering and Computer Science with a certificate in mathematics at the University of Wisconsin-Madison. He is proficient in many programming languages, frameworks, and technologies vastly ranging from low-level C to React-Native javascript frameworks. He is expecting to graduate fall 2017 and is pursuing full employment in the Software industry.

"I think that it's extraordinarily important that we in computer science keep fun in computing. When it started out, it was an awful lot of fun. Of course, the paying customers got shafted every now and then, and after a while we began to take their complaints seriously. We began to feel as if we really were responsible for the successful, error-free perfect use of these machines. I don't think we are. I think we're responsible for stretching them, setting them off in new directions, and keeping fun in the house. I hope the field of computer science never loses its sense of fun. Above all, I hope we don't become missionaries. Don't feel as if you're Bible salesmen. The world has too many of those already. What you know about computing other people will learn. Don't feel as if the key to successful computing is only in your hands. What's in your hands, I think and hope, is intelligence: the ability to see the machine as more than when you were first led up to it, that you can make it more." -Alan Perlis
